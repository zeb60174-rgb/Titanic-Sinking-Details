1. Who Lived and Who Lost Their Lives? (Survival Count)

What it is:

This is a simple bar chart that shows the total number of people who survived the disaster versus those who did not.

The Bars:

You will see two main bars. One represents "0" (those who sadly passed away) and the other represents "1" (those who survived).

The Story:

Looking at the height of the bars, you can immediately see that the bar for those who did not survive is much taller. This tells us right away that more than half of the people on the ship lost their lives.

Why it's important:

It acts as the "big picture" for the whole story, showing us the scale of the tragedy before we look into details like age or ticket price.

2. The "Women and Children First" Rule (Survival by Gender)

What it is:

This is a bar chart that compares how many men and women survived versus how many did not.

The Bars:

It shows side-by-side bars for males and females.

The Story: This chart shows a very clear pattern: even though there were many more men on the ship, a much higher number of women survived.

Why it's important:

It proves that the "women and children first" policy was followed during the emergency, as females had a much higher survival rate than males.

3. How Old Were the Passengers? (Age Distribution)

What it is:

This chart (called a histogram) shows the different age groups of people on the ship, from newborn babies to the elderly. The Shape: The graph looks like a mountain with a high peak. The tallest part of the "mountain" is usually between the ages of 20 and 35.

The Story:

It tells us that the Titanic was mostly filled with young adults. You will also notice a smaller "bump" at the very far left of the chart, which shows that there were quite a few young children and infants on board as well.

Why it's important:

It helps us see the "face" of the passengers. By knowing their ages, we can better understand who was most at risk and how different groups—like children—were prioritized during the rescue.

6. Practice vs. The Real Test (Learning Curve: Training vs. Validation)

What it is:

This line graph shows how a computer model improves as it learns from more data. It tracks two lines: one for Training (how it performs on data it has already seen) and one for Validation (how it performs on "new" data it hasn't seen before).

The Lines:

You will usually see two lines moving across the chart. Ideally, both lines should get closer to a high accuracy score together.

The Story:

It tells us if the model is actually "learning" or just "memorizing". If the Training line is perfect but the Validation line is very low, the model is like a student who memorized the practice test but fails the real exam.

Why it's important:

It helps us find the "sweet spot" where the model is smart enough to handle new, real-world information accurately without making silly mistakes.

7. The "Mistake" Tracker (Neural Network Training Loss)

What it is:

This line graph tracks "Loss," which is just a fancy word for the mistakes the computer makes while learning.

The Curve:

You will usually see a line that starts very high on the left side and slopes downward toward the bottom right.

The Story:

At the beginning, the computer is just guessing and makes many errors (high loss). As it sees the data over and over again, it learns from its mistakes, and the line drops lower and lower.

Why it's important:

It shows us how quickly the computer is "getting it". If the line stops going down and stays flat, it means the computer has learned as much as it can from that specific training session.
